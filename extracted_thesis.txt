ABSTRAK
Pelaksanaan rangkaian 5G New Radio telah meningkatkan kerumitan pengoptimuman Frekuensi Radio (RF) disebabkan oleh penggunaan sistem antena massive MIMO dan persekitaran perambatan isyarat yang sangat dinamik. Pengoptimuman RF secara konvensional banyak bergantung kepada tafsiran manual data ujian pandu (drive test) serta visualisasi liputan statik, yang memakan masa dan bersifat deskriptif. Walaupun model pembelajaran mesin terkini telah meningkatkan ketepatan ramalan dalam mengenal pasti isu liputan, model tersebut tidak mampu memberikan penjelasan semantik atau cadangan preskriptif yang selaras dengan pemikiran kejuruteraan manusia. Selain itu, penggunaan kecerdasan buatan berasaskan awan adalah terhad oleh peraturan kedaulatan data dan privasi, khususnya bagi infrastruktur telekomunikasi negara. Bagi menangani kekangan ini, kajian ini membangunkan sebuah Pembantu Geospatial Pintar berasaskan setempat yang memelihara privasi dengan mengintegrasikan analitik spatial, pembelajaran mesin ramalan, dan Model Bahasa Besar Generatif (LLM) untuk mengautomasikan Analisis Punca Akar (RCA). Sistem ini menggabungkan ukuran ujian pandu lapisan pengguna dengan data infrastruktur rangkaian yang direkodkan oleh peranti dengan ketepatan tinggi bagi membentuk set data berasaskan konteks spatial. Model regresi Random Forest digunakan untuk mengenal pasti kelompok masalah liputan berdasarkan ciri-ciri spatial semata-mata, dengan pencapaian nilai $R^2$ sebanyak 0.82. Seterusnya, LLM bersaiz 4 bilion parameter yang digunakan secara setempat (qwen3.5:4b) memproses kelompok ini untuk menghasilkan penjelasan diagnostik yang boleh difahami oleh manusia. Semua pemprosesan data dan inferens kecerdasan buatan dijalankan sepenuhnya pada perkakasan tempatan, sekali gus memastikan pematuhan ketat terhadap undang-undang kedaulatan data. Keputusan eksperimen menunjukkan bahawa model Random Forest secara signifikan mengatasi algoritma asas dalam ramalan spatial, manakala pengelas RCA berasaskan peraturan mencapai ketepatan sebanyak 99.77% merentasi 2,144 sampel ujian dunia sebenar. Penilaian kualitatif mengesahkan bahawa LLM yang dihoskan secara tempatan berjaya meniru pertimbangan kejuruteraan RF pada tahap pakar, termasuk mendiagnosis isu kompleks seperti pencemaran pilot dan anomali corak antena dengan tepat. Secara keseluruhannya, kajian ini membuktikan bahawa kecerdasan buatan berasaskan edge yang berdaulat mampu menyediakan sokongan keputusan yang praktikal dan boleh ditafsir untuk pengoptimuman 5G tanpa bergantung kepada perkhidmatan awan luaran.
ABSTRACT
The deployment of 5G New Radio networks has increased the complexity of Radio Frequency (RF) optimization due to massive MIMO antenna systems and highly dynamic signal propagation environments. Conventional RF optimization relies heavily on manual interpretation of drive test data and static coverage visualizations, which are time-consuming and largely descriptive. While recent machine learning models have improved predictive accuracy in identifying coverage issues, they cannot provide semantic explanations or prescriptive recommendations that align with human engineering reasoning. Furthermore, the adoption of cloud-based artificial intelligence is constrained by data sovereignty and privacy regulations, particularly for national telecommunications infrastructure. To address these limitations, this study develops a localized, privacy-preserving Intelligent Geospatial Assistant that integrates spatial analytics, predictive machine learning, and a Generative Large Language Model (LLM) to automate Root Cause Analysis (RCA). The system fuses user-plane drive test measurements with highly accurate, device-recorded network infrastructure data to construct a spatially contextualized dataset. A Random Forest regressor is utilized to identify coverage problem clusters based purely on spatial features, achieving an  of 0.82. Subsequently, a locally deployed 4-billion parameter LLM (qwen3.5:4b) processes these clusters to generate explainable, human-readable diagnostic reasoning. All data processing and AI inference occur entirely on local hardware, ensuring strict compliance with data sovereignty laws. Experimental results demonstrate that the Random Forest model significantly outperforms baseline algorithms in spatial prediction, while the rule-based RCA classifier achieves 99.77% accuracy across 2,144 real-world test samples. Qualitative evaluation confirms that the locally hosted LLM successfully emulates expert-level RF engineering judgment, accurately diagnosing complex issues such as pilot pollution and antenna pattern anomalies. Ultimately, this research proves that sovereign, edge-based AI can deliver practical and interpretable decision support for 5G optimization without relying on external cloud services.

INTRODUCTION
Background of the Study
The deployment of 5G New Radio (NR) networks has brought significant complexity into Radio Frequency (RF) optimization. Unlike previous generations, 5G networks utilize massive Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (mMIMO) antenna arrays and higher frequency bands, resulting in highly dynamic signal propagation characteristics that challenge traditional optimization paradigms (Karapantelakis et al., 2024). Historically, RF optimization has relied on manual interpretation of static coverage plots and drive test logs. While these methods effectively visualize signal strength metrics like Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP), they lack the capacity for automated contextual analysis; being purely descriptive, they fail to identify the underlying root causes of performance degradation, such as pilot pollution, overshooting, or terrain shadowing (Al-Ahmed & Shakir, 2022).
The increasing density of 5G deployments in urban environments has further exacerbated this challenge. As the number of small cells and macro sites increases to meet capacity demands, the inter-cell interference environment becomes increasingly complex, requiring sophisticated analytical tools that can process multi-dimensional RF data in a spatially aware manner (Karapantelakis et al., 2024). Traditional drive test analysis tools, while capable of recording detailed RF measurements, provide limited automated diagnostic capability, leaving the burden of interpretation on human engineers who must manually correlate signal degradation patterns with potential physical and environmental causes.
This thesis proposes a novel Intelligent Geospatial Assistant that merges drive test measurements and environmental data to enable automated Root Cause Analysis (RCA). The primary contribution of this study is to determine the feasibility of utilizing Generative AI to emulate the RF engineer's reasoning process by processing datasets that are asynchronously fused through a Local Large Language Model (LLM). This approach addresses the industry's need for intelligent automation that operates within the strict bounds of data sovereignty and privacy, particularly relevant for national infrastructure providers (Othman, 2025).
Problem Statement
Current RF optimization workflows face three critical limitations that hinder operational efficiency and network quality:
Manual interpretation of drive test data remains a labor-intensive and time-consuming process, where engineers must cross-reference RSRP heatmaps with terrain information, antenna configurations, and neighbour lists to diagnose network issues. This approach is not only inefficient but also prone to human error, particularly when identifying transient interference phenomena such as pilot pollution (Al-Ahmed & Shakir, 2022). Previous studies indicate that such analysis can take several hours per route, with diagnostic accuracy largely dependent on the engineer’s level of experience (Azkaei, 2025).
In parallel, although traditional machine learning models such as Random Forest and XGBoost have demonstrated strong capabilities in predictive analytics by identifying areas of weak signal coverage, they lack the ability to provide prescriptive insights (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025; Xu et al., 2025). These models cannot explain the underlying causes of signal degradation in natural language or recommend specific engineering actions, such as antenna downtilt or azimuth adjustments (Xu et al., 2025). Even with the use of explainability techniques like SHAP values, the outputs remain numerical and do not directly translate into actionable decisions (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025). 
Furthermore, the adoption of advanced AI solutions is constrained by data privacy and sovereignty requirements, particularly within the Malaysian telecommunications sector. Operators such as Digital Nasional Berhad (DNB) must comply with strict regulations that limit the use of cloud-based AI platforms like ChatGPT and Gemini due to the risk of exposing critical national infrastructure data (Othman, 2025). Regulatory frameworks, including the Malaysian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010 and the Cyber Security Act 2024, impose stringent controls on data processing and storage, thereby necessitating the use of locally deployed AI systems (Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission, 2024).
There is currently no standardized framework that combines the spatial precision of GIS, the predictive power of Random Forest, and the semantic reasoning of Local LLMs into a unified, privacy-compliant dashboard. This research addresses this gap by developing a localized, privacy-preserving framework that integrates geospatial analytics with AI-driven root cause reasoning for RF optimization.
Research Objectives
The main objectives of this research are:
To develop a data pipeline that fuses user-plane drive test metrics (RSRP, SINR) with highly accurate, device-recorded network infrastructure data (cellinfo.txt) and geospatial environmental layers, overcoming the inaccuracy limitations of crowdsourced databases like OpenCellID.
To assess whether locally deployed Generative AI can provide explainable RF root cause analysis that aligns with standard human engineering judgment under data sovereignty constraints.
To evaluate the integration of predictive geospatial analytics and local Generative AI within a GIS-based decision-support system for RF optimization.

Scope of Research
This research is primarily focused on the optimization of Downlink (DL) coverage within LTE and 5G Non-Standalone (NSA) networks. To capture a realistic representation of the user experience, field measurement data was collected using the fully licensed G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 application installed on a standard Android 5G handset (Infinix Note 60 Pro) (Chatchalermpu et al., 2024). The drive tests were conducted along pre-defined arterial routes in Bandar Utama, Selangor, Malaysia, where a comprehensive database of user-plane metrics under different propagation conditions was developed.
In terms of infrastructure and analysis, the study combines data from multiple sources. Unlike previous approaches that relied on crowdsourced databases such as OpenCellID for base station locations, this research leverages the precise site coordinates recorded directly by G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 in the cellinfo.txt file, providing sub-meter accuracy for spatial fusion calculations. Environmental clutter data is extracted from OpenStreetMap for contextual analysis. For the analytical engine, the backend is Python-based for data processing, using Scikit-Learn for predictive modeling (Random Forest Regressor and Classifier) (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025), and the Ollama framework is utilized to implement the local Large Language Model (qwen3.5:4b) for prescriptive reasoning. The visualization and interaction are conducted through a customized dashboard within QGIS.
The study acknowledges specific technical boundaries to ensure feasibility. It does not extend to Uplink (UL) performance optimization or core network latency analysis. Furthermore, the system's efficacy is evaluated against a strict success criterion. Specifically, the AI-generated root cause analysis must demonstrate qualitative comparability with human engineering judgment and comply with standard industry optimization practices, such as those documented in recent AI/ML frameworks for NR air interfaces (3rd Generation Partnership Project, 2024).
Thesis Organization
This thesis is structured into five chapters, guiding the reader from theoretical foundations to practical implementation and conclusion.
Chapter 2 (Literature Review) critically examines key technologies used in RF optimization, highlighting the existing gap in prescriptive analytics. The growing application of machine learning in wireless network optimization has been widely documented, demonstrating improvements in network performance and cost efficiency (Mughees et al., 2020). The chapter traces the transition from empirical propagation models to data-driven approaches, emphasizing the effectiveness of algorithms such as Random Forest and XGBoost for coverage prediction (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025). It also discusses challenges such as inter-cell interference and pilot pollution in dense 5G environments (Li, 2024), explores density-based spatial clustering for anomaly detection (Mourya et al., 2025), and reviews the emerging integration of Generative AI and GIS for automating engineering reasoning (Songjiayi, 2024).
Chapter 3 (Methodology) details the comprehensive system architecture designed for this study. It elaborates on the data acquisition process through G-NetTrack Pro v33.3, the pre-processing pipeline incorporating Haversine distance calculation, and the configuration of the Local Large Language Model (qwen3.5:4b via Ollama). The chapter also presents the mathematical formulations for the Random Forest Regressor, DBSCAN clustering algorithm, and the Haversine formula, along with the prompt engineering strategies ensuring the AI adheres to RF engineering principles (Sana et al., 2025).
Chapter 4 (Results and Discussion) presents the empirical findings from the Third Drive Test conducted in Bandar Utama. The chapter evaluates the predictive performance of the Random Forest model against baseline alternatives (Linear Regression, KNN, SVR), presents the DBSCAN cluster analysis with PCI-based pilot pollution detection, and discusses the qualitative performance of the AI-generated root cause analysis in comparison to human expert judgment.
Chapter 5 (Conclusion) summarizes the key contributions of the research, discusses its implications for data sovereignty in the telecommunications industry, and offers recommendations for future work, including the potential integration with O-RAN architecture and 6G Digital Twins.
 
LITERATURE REVIEW
Introduction
This chapter reviews existing methodologies in RF optimization, focusing on the transition from empirical models to AI-driven approaches. The deployment of 5G New Radio (NR) networks has rendered traditional drive test analysis increasingly complex due to massive MIMO systems and dynamic propagation environments, necessitating automated and predictive frameworks (Yuliana, 2024). It evaluates the contribution of machine learning algorithms for signal prediction through a critical analysis and recognizes a highly specific gap in "Prescriptive Analytics," namely, the ability to automate engineering reasoning. In keeping up with the rapid progress made in this area, this review relies strongly on publications from the years 2022 to 2025, which feature the developing intersection of Generative AI and telecommunications.
Related Work
This section provides an extensive analysis of key research papers and technical domains that form the foundation of the proposed framework. These projects represent the state-of-the-art in Predictive AI, Explainable AI (XAI), spatial clustering, interference mitigation, and the first applications of Generative AI in telecommunications.
Comparison of Machine Learning Algorithms for 5G Coverage Prediction (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025)
Maneesha and Kiran (2025) conducted a comprehensive study to identify the most effective machine learning algorithm for predicting 5G coverage areas. Their research addressed the difficult circumstances in the case of the transmission of a 5G signal, which is related to the fact that existing empirical models (such as COST-231) cannot adequately account for the dense, non-linear multipath effects of urban environments.
Their study utilized real-world RF datasets incorporating parameters such as Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP) and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), and evaluated several supervised learning techniques, including Logistic Regression, Support Vector Machines (SVM), and Random Forest. 
The findings demonstrated that Random Forest significantly outperformed the other models, achieving a coefficient of determination (R²) of 0.97 and a Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of 1.14 dB, highlighting its robustness in handling noisy and high-dimensional drive-test data compared to single-estimator approaches. 
Despite its strong predictive capability—supporting its adoption in Phase 3 of this thesis—the Random Forest model remains limited as a “black box” in terms of interpretability, as it can accurately predict coverage degradation without providing insight into underlying causes, such as antenna downtilt or environmental clutter, nor offering actionable recommendations. This limitation underscores the research gap that the prescriptive analytics capabilities of locally deployed LLMs aim to address.
Path Loss Prediction Model Based on XGBoost and SHAP (Xu et al., 2025) 
Xu et al. (2025) addressed the "Black Box" issue regarding the opacity of machine learning models. Understanding that engineers prefer physics-based explanations over unqualified predictions, they combined XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) and SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to predict and explain 5G path loss. 
The methodology combined drive test measurements with 3D building data, where XGBoost was employed for prediction and SHAP values were used to quantify the contribution of key features such as building height, distance, and clutter type to overall signal degradation. The results showed that the XGBoost model achieved strong predictive performance with an R² greater than 0.9, while SHAP analysis identified “Distance to Site” and “Building Height” as the dominant factors influencing signal loss in non-line-of-sight (NLOS) conditions. 
Although this approach significantly improves model interpretability, its outputs remain primarily mathematical rather than semantic. For example, identifying that building height contributes +15 dB to path loss is informative but does not directly translate into actionable engineering decisions. Engineers must still interpret these values to determine appropriate actions, such as adjusting antenna tilt. This limitation highlights the need for more intuitive, human-readable insights, which this thesis addresses by leveraging a locally deployed LLM to convert feature importance into clear engineering recommendations.
Semi-Supervised Learning for Coverage Hole Detection (Al-Ahmed & Shakir, 2022)
In the context of Self-Organizing Networks (SON), Al-Ahmed and Shakir (2022) presented a semi-supervised learning model for identifying coverage holes using Minimization of Drive Tests (MDT) data data, addressing the common challenge of limited labeled failure data in real-world networks. 
Their methodology utilized a small set of labeled coverage hole instances to train a classifier, which was then applied to detect similar patterns within large volumes of unlabeled user data. The findings demonstrated that this approach outperformed traditional fixed-threshold methods by effectively identifying weak coverage areas without the need for extensive manual drive testing. 
However, despite its effectiveness in detection, the model lacks diagnostic depth, as it clusters poor coverage points without distinguishing their underlying causes, such as pilot pollution, overshooting, or shadowing. As a result, network operators are alerted to issues but are not provided with actionable solutions. This limitation is addressed in the proposed thesis by incorporating cluster-level spatial features into a Generative AI framework, enabling the differentiation of RF fault types through contextual spatial reasoning.
Reasoning Language Models for Root Cause Analysis (Sana et al., 2025)
Sana et al. (2025), affiliated with Huawei’s research unit, explored the use of existing large language models, including GPT-4, for Root Cause Analysis (RCA) in 5G wireless networks by guiding pre-trained models through structured prompting rather than developing new architectures. 
Their methodology involved constructing a domain-specific dataset known as “TeleLogs,” consisting of real network fault logs, and applying Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting to lead LLMs through step-by-step diagnostic reasoning. The study also compared general-purpose models with domain-adapted variants to assess the impact of telecommunications-specific fine-tuning. 
The findings indicated that CoT prompting significantly reduced hallucination in technical diagnosis and enabled accurate identification of configuration mismatches from textual logs, although general-purpose models without domain adaptation struggled with unfamiliar network-specific terminology. 
While this work represents a closely related effort in applying LLMs to RF diagnostics, it presents two key limitations. First, the reliance on cloud-based models or high-performance GPU infrastructure raises data sovereignty concerns, which are not suitable for national telecommunications operators such as Digital Nasional Berhad (DNB). Second, the approach is limited to textual log inputs and does not incorporate spatial context, preventing the system from reasoning about the geographic origin or environmental causes of faults. This thesis addresses these limitations by deploying a quantized LLM (qwen3.5:4b) entirely on local consumer-grade hardware using the Ollama framework, and by enriching the model input with spatially derived features—such as Haversine distance, SINR, and PCI count—based on real-world drive test data, thereby enabling geospatially aware and privacy-preserving diagnostic recommendations.
Real-Time Overshoot and Undershoot Detection in Cellular Networks
As 5G networks evolve toward higher cell densities to meet increasing capacity demands, inter-cell interference has become a major limitation to overall network performance. The overlap of multiple cell coverage areas often results in pilot pollution, where User Equipment (UE) detects several strong signals (Physical Cell Identities, PCIs) simultaneously, leading to degraded Signal-to-Interference-plus-Noise Ratio (SINR) even when the Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP) remains strong (Siddiqui et al., 2021). 
In this context, Trujillo et al. (2025) investigated the detection of interference-related phenomena, particularly overshoot, where cells interfere with neighboring coverage areas, and undershoot, which results in coverage gaps. Their methodology focused on real-time detection using machine learning models applied to Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) collected from operational networks, aiming to automate anomaly identification without relying on manual drive test analysis. 
The findings demonstrated that the proposed models could effectively distinguish between overshoot, undershoot, and normal network conditions, achieving a precision of 98.42% and a test accuracy of 88.66%, while also highlighting the importance of interference analysis in optimizing antenna tilt and improving network performance. 
However, despite its effectiveness in anomaly detection, the framework remains limited to diagnostic capabilities, as it identifies the presence of issues without generating actionable, natural language recommendations for resolution. Additionally, its reliance on network-side KPIs lacks the fine-grained spatial detail provided by user-plane drive test data. This thesis addresses these limitations by incorporating high-resolution spatial measurements, including RSRP, SINR, and PCI, to detect pilot pollution more precisely, and by leveraging a locally deployed LLM to produce prescriptive, context-aware engineering recommendations.
Machine Learning-Based Anomaly Detection and Root Cause Analysis in Mobile Networks
The shift from point-based analysis to area-based optimization in RF engineering necessitates the use of robust spatial clustering techniques. Conventional approaches such as K-Means are often unsuitable for geospatial RF data, as they require predefined cluster counts and assume spherical cluster distributions. To overcome these limitations, researchers frequently adopt the Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) algorithm, which has proven highly effective for spatial grouping in modern wireless networks (Mourya et al., 2025). 
In this context, Nikula et al. (2025) applied DBSCAN for anomaly detection in modern mobile networks through an automated machine learning framework designed to analyze unlabeled data. Their methodology integrated DBSCAN with Long Short-Term Memory Autoencoders (LSTM-AE), where DBSCAN was specifically used to distinguish normal from anomalous network behavior without requiring prior knowledge of cluster numbers. 
The findings confirmed that DBSCAN is highly effective in identifying contiguous regions of network degradation, as it groups data points based on local density rather than distance to a centroid, making it well-suited for irregular and non-spherical patterns such as street canyons or building-induced signal shadows. 
Despite these strengths, the study primarily focused on temporal KPI-based patterns rather than spatial coordinates such as latitude and longitude, and its root cause analysis relied on statistical inference rather than semantic interpretation. Building on this foundation, the present thesis applies DBSCAN directly within the geospatial domain to cluster areas of degraded RSRP and enhances the analysis by incorporating a generative local LLM, enabling context-aware reasoning across spatially coherent problem regions instead of isolated data points.

The Gap in Prescriptive Analytics
A review of similar projects shows that existing research tends to fall into two main directions: studies led by human expert analysis and those driven by automated, AI-based approaches. Researchers such as Maneesha & Kiran (2025) and Xu et al. (2025) excel at numerical prediction but lack semantic reasoning. Conversely, studies like Sana et al. (2025) introduce reasoning but often rely on cloud infrastructure and text logs, neglecting the spatial "where" and "why" of RF propagation. The International Telecommunication Union (ITU-T, 2025) has recognized the deployment of Generative AI in telecom networks as a critical emerging requirement, yet standard frameworks for local execution remain scarce. Furthermore, the transition from traditional automated self-healing concepts to modern ML-driven anomaly detection (Azkaei, 2025)  has been hindered by a lack of spatial context in diagnostic models.
Additionally, a significant limitation in prior spatial modeling research has been the reliance on crowdsourced infrastructure databases. Many previous studies utilized OpenCellID to estimate base station locations for distance calculations. However, OpenCellID relies on crowdsourced triangulation, which suffers from poor spatial accuracy (often exhibiting mean errors exceeding 1 kilometer) and severe under-representation of 5G NR sites (Infovista, 2021). This inaccuracy fundamentally compromises any spatial predictive model. The methodology proposed in this thesis overcomes this limitation by extracting highly accurate, GPS-locked site coordinates directly from the cellinfo.txt file recorded by the UE during the drive test, ensuring sub-meter precision for spatial fusion.
Integration of GIS and AI
Since RF optimization depends strongly on spatial information, close integration between AI models and GIS becomes necessary. Distance is a fundamental determinant of free-space path loss in RF propagation (Rappaport, 2024), making geospatial context essential for any diagnostic engine. Songjiayi (2024) detailed a workflow integrating QGIS with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for landscape planning. This thesis modifies that approach for use in the telecommunications sector. By treating the RF environment as a "landscape" of signal interference, QGIS can serve not just as a map, but as the interactive "context engine" that feeds the Local LLM the necessary spatial data (e.g., "The user is in a valley, 500m from the tower with 11 overlapping PCIs") to generate accurate, context-aware engineering advice.

METHODOLOGY
Introduction
This chapter outlines the research methodology employed to develop the "Intelligent Geospatial Assistant." The study follows the Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining (CRISP-DM) framework, a widely adopted industry-standard lifecycle for data science projects (Devi, P. R., 2026). 
The methodology is structured in alignment with the CRISP-DM lifecycle, where data understanding and preparation are realized through G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 integration and spatial fusion, modeling and evaluation are executed within the intelligent core using Random Forest and DBSCAN algorithms, and deployment is operationalized through prescriptive analytics output within QGIS. These components are implemented in a systematic and sequential manner, from business understanding through to deployment.
All AI reasoning in this study is executed locally without reliance on external cloud services, ensuring full compliance with data sovereignty and privacy requirements as articulated by the Malaysian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010 (Othman, 2025).
Proposed Framework
The research framework designed for this study moves beyond traditional linear data processing by establishing a multi-layered intelligent architecture. Unlike standard optimization workflows that rely solely on the manual interpretation of static heatmaps, this framework presents an Integrated Geospatial-Semantic Engine that embodies automated reasoning. The framework is organized into three distinct functional layers: (1) Data Acquisition & Spatial Fusion, (2) The Sovereign Intelligent Core, and (3) Prescriptive Analytics Output.
Layer 1: Data Acquisition and Spatial Fusion
The foundational layer addresses the problem of fragmented network data. Standard drive tests typically deliver isolated user-plane metrics (RSRP, SINR) without environmental context. As a significant contribution, a Spatial Fusion Mechanism is designed to automatically synchronize dynamic telemetry data from the Infinix Note 60 Pro with the highly accurate, device-recorded static network topology found in the cellinfo.txt file. This mechanism programmatically links raw signal data to physical tower locations, thereby creating the necessary spatial context for intelligent diagnosis.
This layer extends conventional drive-test analysis by calculating derived geospatial features. It utilizes deterministic spatial computations developed in Python to generate geospatial features, most notably the Haversine Distance to the Serving Cell (). Layer 2: The Sovereign Intelligent Core (Primary Contribution)
This layer represents the core innovation of the research, effectively bridging the gap between predictive AI, driven by machine learning, and prescriptive AI, enabled through generative reasoning, while maintaining strict compliance with data sovereignty requirements. The deployment of AI models on edge hardware reflects a growing trend in telecommunications, where operators utilize localized computing to reduce latency and safeguard data privacy (Karapantelakis et al., 2024). The architecture is composed of three sequential processing engines.
The first component, the Spatial Predictive Engine, utilizes a Random Forest Regressor to model and predict signal strength based solely on spatial inputs. Its primary objective is to capture the non-linear relationship between terrain characteristics, environmental clutter, and RSRP using geographic features such as Latitude, Longitude, and Haversine Distance. The second component, the Spatial Clustering Engine, employs the Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) algorithm to identify contiguous regions of network degradation. This is achieved by clustering data points where RSRP falls below a defined threshold (e.g., < -95 dBm). The final component, the Sovereign Generative Reasoning Engine, constitutes the central prescriptive layer of the framework. It is built upon a locally deployed Large Language Model, specifically qwen3.5:4b, hosted via the Ollama framework on consumer-grade hardware. This engine processes aggregated statistical summaries of each identified problem cluster, including metrics such as average RSRP, minimum SINR, and PCI count, and generates expert-level root cause diagnoses using Context-Aware Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting. Importantly, all processing is performed locally, ensuring that no sensitive data is transmitted to external cloud services, thereby preserving full compliance with data sovereignty requirements (Othman, 2025). This approach aligns with emerging industry guidance on deploying LLMs at the network edge, where local inference reduces both latency and data exposure risks (GSMA, 2025).
Layer 3: Prescriptive Analytics Output
The final layer illustrates the conversion of the AI's output into actionable engineering insights. In comparison to traditional tools that only display descriptive statistics (e.g., "Poor Coverage"), this framework introduces a novel visualization of Prescriptive Diagnoses integrated directly into a QGIS Dashboard.
This layer generates a structured Root Cause Analysis (RCA) Report that maps specific geographic clusters to their probable root causes (e.g., "Interference due to Pilot Pollution" vs. "Weak Coverage due to physical shadowing"), enabling engineers to transition seamlessly from visualization to remediation.
While this section presents the conceptual framework of the proposed system, the concrete system architecture and execution flow are detailed in Section 3.5.
Project Requirements
The development of the Intelligent Geospatial Assistant requires a specific hardware and software platform capable of supporting both intensive geospatial processing and local AI inference.
Hardware Requirements
The experimental setup for this study consists of both mobile equipment for data collection and a workstation environment for processing. For drive test data collection, an Infinix Note 60 Pro smartphone was utilized. The device is equipped with an internal GPS module and a 5G cellular modem, enabling accurate geolocation tagging and real-time network measurement during field testing using G-NetTrack Pro v33.3.
Data processing, spatial analysis, and AI-based reasoning were conducted on a consumer-grade workstation: a Gigabyte G5 laptop powered by an 11th Generation Intel® Core™ i5-11400H processor with 24 GB of RAM, running Windows 11. Crucially, the system is equipped with an NVIDIA® GeForce RTX™ 3050 laptop GPU (4GB VRAM), enabling CUDA-accelerated computation to support local large language model (LLM) inference when deployed via Ollama.
This hardware setup proved sufficient to execute GPU-accelerated geospatial processing within QGIS and perform quantized local LLM inference without reliance on external cloud infrastructure.
Software Tools & Technology Stack
From a software perspective, the fully licensed G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 Android application is used for collecting RF field measurement data, recording essential parameters such as RSRP, PCI, SINR, and highly accurate serving cell coordinates in the cellinfo.txt file (Chatchalermpu et al., 2024). QGIS (version 3.28 Firenze or newer) serves as the primary user interface and spatial analysis platform for geospatial processing and visualization.
Language inference at the local level is enabled through Ollama, an open-source framework for running large language models exclusively on local hardware. The selected model is qwen3.5:4b (4 billion parameters), quantized to 4 bits, chosen specifically to balance robust engineering reasoning capability with the strict VRAM constraints of the local hardware.
Python (version 3.9 or newer) powers the backend of the system, utilizing the following libraries: pandas for data manipulation, scikit-learn for Random Forest modeling and DBSCAN clustering, requests for local API communication with Ollama, and PyQGIS for direct integration with the QGIS environment.
Data Design
The proposed system operates by merging two primary data sources generated simultaneously during the drive test: a dynamic user-plane dataset and a static network topology dataset.

Data Pre-processing Pipeline
The raw data is processed through a structured pre-processing pipeline prior to analysis. First, corrupted entries (such as RSRP readings of -340 dBm or missing coordinates) are removed. Stationary samples are excluded by filtering measurements with speeds below a defined threshold, removing congestion-induced artifacts while preserving representative mobility conditions.
Subsequently, a spatial join operation is executed using Python, where each drive test measurement is matched to its corresponding serving base station coordinates derived from the cellinfo.txt file based on the active PCI. This enables the calculation of the primary derived feature: the Haversine Distance to the serving site.
System Architecture and Intelligent Core Design
The system is built around a modular client–server architecture that ensures both functionality and data remain under strict local control. QGIS acts as the front-end environment, providing interactive spatial visualizations and tools for user engagement. A custom Python script (geospatial_ai_bot.py) acts as the middleware (Kshetri & Ojha, 2025), executing the machine learning pipeline (Random Forest and DBSCAN) and formatting the results. Ollama runs locally as the AI inference engine hosting the qwen3.5:4b language model.
These components communicate through a local REST interface (http://localhost:11434), exchanging structured JSON messages without ever transmitting data over the external internet. Consequently, all RF measurements, derived spatial features, and AI-generated outputs remain entirely on-premises. This design satisfies data sovereignty regulations while delivering automated, intelligent RF diagnostics.
The reasoning process within the system's intelligent core relies heavily on structured Prompt Engineering. The process begins with the spatially aware clusters generated by DBSCAN. For each cluster, a statistical profile is generated, including the number of points, average RSRP, minimum SINR, average distance to the site, and crucially, the number of unique PCIs detected within the cluster (an indicator of pilot pollution).
These profiles are injected into a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompt template. The template explicitly instructs the LLM to act as a Senior 5G RF Optimization Engineer and guides its reasoning process step-by-step:

System Prompt: You are a Senior 5G RF Optimization Engineer. Analyze the following drive test cluster data and determine the root cause of the degradation.

User Prompt:
Cluster ID: {cluster_id}
Average RSRP: {avg_rsrp} dBm
Minimum SINR: {min_sinr} dB
Average Distance to Site: {avg_distance} m
Unique PCIs detected: {pci_count} (List: {pci_list})

Instructions:
1. Analyze the RSRP vs Distance relationship. Is it a coverage hole?
2. Analyze the SINR and unique PCI count. Is there pilot pollution/interference?
3. Provide a clear Root Cause classification.
4. Recommend 3 specific engineering actions to resolve the issue.
The prompt is submitted to the local qwen3.5:4b model via Ollama, which produces a structured root-cause assessment. Because the reasoning logic is cleanly separated from the spatial processing, the framework supports transparent, repeatable, and highly interpretable RF diagnostics.
 Experiment Setup
Phase 1: Drive Test Data Collection
In phase one, real-world data was collected within the designated study area of Bandar Utama, Selangor. Data was collected using G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 on the Infinix Note 60 Pro while traveling through arterial roads. The mobile device was mounted on the dashboard to ensure unobstructed GPS reception, with measurements recorded at one-second intervals. This configuration captures typical user experience patterns, with particular emphasis on handover zones and inter-cell boundaries (Chatchalermpu et al., 2024).
Phase 2: QGIS Environment Configuration
In phase two, the QGIS environment is established by creating a specific project file and importing OpenStreetMap base layers to provide geographic context. The output from the Python machine learning pipeline (ML_Predicted_RCA.csv) is imported as a Delimited Text Layer. RSRP visual symbology is applied to depict signal strength gradients, while the problem clusters and base station locations (Site_Locations.csv) are overlaid as distinct layers, allowing users to visually correlate coverage holes with network topology.
Phase 3: AI Integration (QGIS to Ollama)
In the final phase, the integration between the spatial data and the local AI is executed. Ollama is installed locally and configured to serve the qwen3.5:4b model on the default local port. The custom QGIS plugin (Intelligent Geospatial Assistant) connects to the local AI engine via HTTP POST requests. The plugin dynamically constructs JSON prompts containing the summarized RF cluster features and submits them to Ollama's generation endpoint. The resulting diagnostic texts are parsed and displayed directly within the QGIS interface, enabling context-aware engineering diagnostics seamlessly integrated into the geospatial workflow (Songjiayi, 2024).
Potential Use of Solution
This solution is designed for specific stakeholders in the telecommunications industry:
RF Optimization Engineers: Can utilize the system to automate the tedious process of analyzing drive test logs, rapidly identifying interference scenarios (such as pilot pollution) and receiving immediate, context-aware mitigation strategies.
Network Planners: Can leverage the aggregated cluster analysis to direct physical site adjustments (e.g., antenna tilts) or justify the deployment of additional infrastructure to resolve persistent coverage holes.
National Infrastructure Providers: Organizations such as Digital Nasional Berhad (DNB) can deploy the solution for comprehensive network optimization while strictly adhering to data sovereignty regulations and the PDPA, ensuring that critical national infrastructure data is never exposed to external cloud platforms (Othman, 2025).

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Introduction
This chapter presents the empirical findings derived from the deployment of the proposed machine learning framework for automated geospatial root cause analysis in 5G Radio Frequency (RF) optimization. The framework addresses critical challenges in modern 5G network management, where operators must balance performance optimization with operational efficiency (Shaibu et al., 2025).
As highlighted in Chapter 2, no established framework currently integrates the spatial accuracy of GIS, the predictive capabilities of Random Forest, and the semantic reasoning of locally deployed LLMs within a single privacy-compliant dashboard (Sana et al., 2025; Songjiayi, 2024). The evaluation presented herein is grounded in real-world 5G Non-Standalone (NSA) drive test data collected using G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 on an Infinix Note 60 Pro device in Bandar Utama, Selangor, Malaysia.
The results are systematically organized to address the three research objectives outlined in Chapter 1. First, the data collection and descriptive statistics of the RF environment are detailed. Second, the performance of the Random Forest (RF) Regressor in predicting Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP) based on purely spatial features is evaluated, including a rigorous baseline comparison against alternative machine learning models (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025). 
Third, the efficacy of the Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) algorithm in identifying localized coverage anomalies is presented (Mourya et al., 2025; Zhang, H., 2023). Finally, the outcomes of the rule-based Root Cause Analysis (RCA) and the integration of locally deployed Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) for prescriptive engineering recommendations are discussed within the context of data sovereignty (Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission, 2024).
Data Collection and Descriptive Statistics
The primary dataset for this study was obtained through an extensive drive test conducted on March 14, 2026. Unlike previous iterations that relied on outdated software versions, the data was captured using the fully licensed G-NetTrack Pro v33.3, which accurately recorded serving site locations (Latitude and Longitude) directly into the cellinfo.txt file. This enhancement eliminated the reliance on third-party databases such as OpenCelliD, significantly improving the spatial accuracy of the Haversine distance calculations (Infovista, 2021).
A total of 2,146 raw measurement samples were collected. After data cleaning—which involved filtering out invalid GPS coordinates and anomalous RSRP values (e.g., -340 dBm)—a robust dataset of 2,144 valid samples was established. The network environment was predominantly 5G New Radio (NR), accounting for 93.4% (2,003 samples) of the measurements, with the remaining 6.6% (141 samples) operating on 4G LTE. The primary frequency band utilized was the N78 band (3500 MHz Time Division Duplex), representing 92.7% of the total data.

Predictive Modeling of RSRP using Random Forest Regressor
The Random Forest Regressor serves as the Spatial Intelligence Engine (the "WHERE" component) of the proposed framework. Its purpose is strictly predictive: to model the spatial distribution of RSRP across the drive test area and to identify geographic locations exhibiting substandard coverage. As articulated in Chapter 1, traditional machine learning models excel at Predictive Analytics—forecasting where a signal will be weak—but inherently lack Prescriptive Analytics capabilities; they cannot explain why a signal is weak or recommend specific physical adjustments (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025; Xu et al., 2025). This deliberate limitation is what necessitates the subsequent integration of the local LLM (Section 4.7).
The model was trained to predict RSRP based exclusively on three spatial features: Latitude, Longitude, and Haversine Distance to the serving site. The dataset was split into an 80% training set (1,715 samples) and a 20% testing set (429 samples).

Problem Cluster Identification via DBSCAN
While predicting overall network coverage is valuable, RF optimization requires the isolation of specific geographic areas exhibiting substandard performance. To achieve this, the DBSCAN algorithm was applied to the dataset (Mourya et al., 2025; Li, Q., 2023). DBSCAN is particularly suited for geospatial anomaly detection as it does not require the a priori specification of the number of clusters and can identify clusters of arbitrary shape while filtering out isolated noise points (Azkaei, 2025).
The clustering was constrained to points exhibiting poor coverage, defined by an RSRP threshold of <-95 dBm. The DBSCAN hyperparameters were set to an epsilon (ε) radius of 150 metres and a minimum samples threshold of 3 points, selected to detect localized degradation zones without over-segmenting sparse data — consistent with spatial clustering approaches employed in self-organizing network (SON) anomaly detection (Canastro et al., 2021).
Out of the 2,144 valid samples, 561 points (26.2%) fell below the -95 dBm threshold. The DBSCAN algorithm successfully grouped these points into 7 distinct problem clusters. Automated Root Cause Analysis Labelling
Following the identification of problem clusters, a rule-based Root Cause Analysis (RCA) algorithm was applied to classify the specific nature of the RF degradation for each data point. The logic was designed to emulate the deductive reasoning of an RF engineer, utilizing RSRP, SINR, and Haversine Distance as the three discriminating metrics (Inyang & Johnson, 2025). The classification rules were defined as follows:
Normal Coverage: RSRP -95 dBm.
Coverage Hole (Distance): RSRP < -95 dBm AND Distance > 500 m. This rule captures areas where signal degradation is primarily attributable to free-space path loss, as the UE is beyond the effective cell radius.
Interference (Pilot Pollution): RSRP < -95$ dBm AND Distance 500 m AND SINR < 0 dB. This rule identifies locations where the UE is within reasonable range of the site but experiences severe inter-cell interference, indicated by the SINR falling below 0 dB (i.e., the interference power exceeds the desired signal power).
Weak Coverage (Antenna Pattern / Shadowing): RSRP < -95$ dBm AND Distance 500 m AND SINR dB. This rule captures locations where the signal is weak despite proximity to the site and the absence of interference, pointing to physical obstructions (shadowing) or antenna pattern deficiencies.

Validation of RCA Logic via Classifier
To validate the robustness of the identified spatial and RF relationships, a Random Forest classifier was developed to predict RCA labels using Latitude, Longitude, RSRP, and Haversine Distance as input features. The model achieved a test accuracy of 99.77%, indicating strong predictive performance. It is important to note that the classifier was trained on rule-generated labels rather than manually annotated ground truth. As such, the near-perfect accuracy reflects the clear separability of patterns within the rule-defined feature space, rather than independent validation against human-labeled data. Nevertheless, the results demonstrate that the combined use of spatial positioning, distance metrics, and signal strength forms a comprehensive and reliable signature for diagnosing network faults.

Integration of Local Generative AI for Prescriptive Analytics
To comply with the strict data sovereignty regulations mandated by the Malaysian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010 and the Cyber Security Act 2024, a local Large Language Model (LLM) was deployed (Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission, 2024; Othman, 2025). The qwen3.5:4b model was hosted locally via the Ollama framework, ensuring that sensitive telecommunications infrastructure data—such as exact site coordinates and performance metrics—never traversed the public internet (Pereira et al., 2022).
The Intelligent Geospatial Bot utilized Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting to analyze the aggregated data for each of the 7 problem clusters. By structuring the prompt to include the cluster's location, average RSRP, minimum RSRP, average SINR, minimum SINR, distance to site, unique PCIs, and the rule-based RCA classification, the LLM was guided to produce technically precise, context-aware optimization steps (Wei et al., 2022). The inclusion of both RSRP and SINR in the prompt is critical: it provides the LLM with sufficient information to distinguish between coverage-limited and interference-limited scenarios, enabling differentiated prescriptive recommendations.
LLM Evaluation Case Studies
To evaluate the qualitative performance of the Generative AI, its recommendations were compared against standard RF engineering practices. The ability of the LLM to provide explicit reasoning for its recommendations enhances the interpretability and trustworthiness of the system, which is essential for adoption in critical telecommunications infrastructure (Chai et al., 2024). Two distinct case studies are presented below.
Case Study 1: Interference Mitigation (Cluster 5)
When analyzing Cluster 5 (Avg RSRP: -102.2 dBm, Avg SINR: 2.6 dB, Min SINR: -9.0 dB, Distance: 363.1m, Unique PCIs: 11, Classified as Interference — Severe Pilot Pollution), the local LLM successfully deduced that the combination of poor RSRP, critically low SINR, and 11 unique PCIs at a medium distance definitively indicates severe inter-cell interference (pilot pollution) compounded by physical shadowing. The LLM's generated output recommended specific actions:
Physical Tuning: Adjust the electrical downtilt (e-tilt) of the serving cell (PCI 384) upward by 1-2 degrees to extend the main lobe coverage into the shadowed area, while simultaneously increasing the downtilt of the dominant interfering neighbor to reduce the overlapping footprint causing the pilot pollution.
Parameter Tuning: Review and potentially lower the RsrpThreshold for inter-frequency handover to prevent premature dropping of the connection before the UE can clear the physical obstruction.
Carrier Aggregation: Delay the activation of the Secondary Component Carrier (SCC) until the Primary Cell (PCell) RSRP recovers above -90 dBm, as attempting CA in a -102 dBm environment with SINR of 2.6 dB will result in high block error rates (BLER) and wasted scheduling resources.
Case Study 2: Near-Field Coverage Optimization (Cluster 6)
In contrast, when analyzing Cluster 6 (Avg RSRP: -101.0 dBm, Avg SINR: 16.5 dB, Distance: 48.2m, Unique PCIs: 1), the LLM correctly identified that the high SINR (16.5 dB) and single PCI presence eliminates interference as a root cause. The LLM reasoned that at only 48 meters from the site with no interference, the poor RSRP must be caused by the UE being positioned directly beneath the antenna (in the "null" zone of the vertical radiation pattern). The recommendation was to increase the mechanical downtilt or adjust the vertical beamwidth to fill the near-field coverage gap—a fundamentally different prescription from Cluster 5.
This outcome demonstrates that small, quantized models (such as the 4-billion parameter Qwen 3.5) operating on consumer-grade edge hardware (laptop with RTX 3050) possess sufficient domain-specific reasoning capabilities to generate expert-level RF optimization strategies. The framework effectively bridges the gap between descriptive spatial clustering and prescriptive network engineering, fulfilling the research objectives while strictly adhering to data privacy constraints. Summary
This chapter demonstrated that the proposed Intelligent Geospatial Assistant framework successfully achieved the research objectives. The data pipeline accurately fused drive test measurements with spatial features using the Haversine formula. The Random Forest Regressor demonstrated strong predictive capability ( = 0.8206), significantly outperforming baseline models such as Linear Regression and SVR. The DBSCAN algorithm successfully identified 7 localized degradation zones, and the rule-based RCA classifier accurately categorized network faults with 99.77% accuracy. CONCLUSION
Introduction
This chapter concludes the research by summarizing the key findings derived from the development and deployment of the machine learning framework for automated geospatial root cause analysis in 5G Radio Frequency (RF) optimization. It evaluates the extent to which the research objectives were achieved, highlights the primary contributions to the field of telecommunications engineering, and outlines the limitations encountered during the study. Finally, it provides actionable recommendations for future research and practical implementations in the telecommunications industry.
Summary of Findings
The central premise of this research was to address the inefficiencies inherent in manual 5G drive test analysis by introducing an automated, geospatial, and AI-driven framework. Building upon the research gap established in Chapter 2, this study integrates the predictive capability of Random Forest, the spatial precision of GIS, and the semantic reasoning of a locally deployed LLM into a unified, privacy-compliant framework — a combination not previously realized in the literature (Sana et al., 2025; Songjiayi, 2024). The empirical results, based on the Third Drive Test conducted in Bandar Utama using G-NetTrack Pro v33.3, yielded several significant findings.
First, the Random Forest Regressor demonstrated strong effectiveness in predicting Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP) using only spatial inputs—Latitude, Longitude, and Haversine Distance—achieving a testing  of 0.8206 and a Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of 4.57 dBm. This result confirms that spatial coordinates in dense urban environments inherently capture the impact of physical structures, such as building shadowing, on RF propagation, thereby enabling accurate prediction without reliance on computationally intensive 3D ray-tracing models (Yuliana, 2024; Fauzi et al., 2022). 
Second, the combined use of RSRP and SINR proved critical for accurate root cause classification, as RSRP reflects signal strength while SINR indicates signal quality relative to interference and noise. Their joint analysis enabled differentiation between distance-related path loss (low RSRP, normal SINR), interference-driven degradation (low RSRP, low SINR), and antenna-related anomalies (low RSRP, high SINR at short range), consistent with multi-dimensional evaluation standards outlined in 3GPP TS 38.133 and supported by Nurakhov (2025) (3rd Generation Partnership Project, 2023a; Nurakhov, 2025).
Third, the application of the Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise (DBSCAN) algorithm successfully shifted the analysis from isolated measurements to contiguous problem regions, identifying seven clusters of poor coverage (RSRP $< -95$ dBm) and enabling targeted, large-scale optimization strategies aligned with self-organizing network (SON) principles (Mourya et al., 2025; Zhang, H., 2023). 
Fourth, the implementation of a rule-based root cause classification framework, utilizing RSRP, SINR, and Haversine Distance as discriminative features, effectively categorized RF degradation patterns, revealing that 14.5% of the network area experienced weak coverage due to antenna patterns or physical obstructions, 9.2% due to distance-related coverage gaps, and 2.5% due to interference such as pilot pollution. The robustness of this approach was further validated using a Random Forest Classifier that achieved 99.77% accuracy, confirming the reliability of fundamental RF metrics for automated diagnostics (Inyang & Johnson, 2025;  Li, Q., 2023). 
Finally, the deployment of a local Large Language Model (LLM), specifically qwen3.5:4b via the Ollama framework, demonstrated that Generative AI can deliver expert-level, actionable engineering recommendations—such as antenna tilt adjustments and Carrier Aggregation configurations—while preserving strict data sovereignty. The LLM generated differentiated recommendations based on RSRP-SINR relationships, providing interference mitigation strategies for interference-limited clusters and physical optimization solutions for coverage-limited areas. By ensuring that all data processing occurs on local consumer-grade hardware, the framework complies fully with the Malaysian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010, offering a practical and compliant pathway for integrating AI into regulated telecommunications environments (Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission, 2024), and aligning with emerging governance standards where data privacy and residency are essential (Othman, 2025).
Achievement of Research Objectives
The outcomes of this study successfully fulfilled the research objectives established in Chapter 1:
Objective 1: A robust data pipeline was developed that successfully fused user-plane drive test metrics (RSRP, SINR) with network infrastructure data. The use of the Haversine formula to calculate the exact distance between the User Equipment (UE) and the serving base station provided a critical spatial feature that drove the predictive accuracy of the machine learning models. The pipeline processed 2,144 valid samples with zero data loss.
Objective 2: The research conclusively assessed and validated that locally deployed Generative AI can provide explainable RF root cause analysis. The Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting guided the LLM to produce technical diagnostics that aligned with standard human engineering judgment, while the local deployment architecture ensured absolute data sovereignty. The LLM successfully generated differentiated recommendations based on the RSRP-SINR profile of each cluster.
Objective 3: The integration of predictive geospatial analytics (Random Forest and DBSCAN) and local Generative AI within a GIS-based decision-support system was successfully evaluated. The development of the Intelligent Geospatial Assistant QGIS plugin provided a tangible, visual interface for RF engineers to interact with the automated insights, thereby streamlining the optimization workflow.
Contributions to Knowledge
This research contributes significantly to the intersection of telecommunications engineering and artificial intelligence across methodological, practical, and regulatory dimensions. 
From a methodological perspective, it introduces a novel end-to-end framework that bridges the gap between descriptive spatial clustering and prescriptive Generative AI, extending beyond traditional coverage prediction (the “WHERE”) to include automated, natural-language engineering insights that explain the “WHY” and “HOW TO FIX.” In doing so, it demonstrates that the predictive capabilities of Random Forest and the semantic reasoning of a locally deployed LLM are complementary rather than competing approaches (Maneesha & Kiran, 2025). 
Practically, the development of the open-source QGIS plugin, known as the Intelligent Geospatial Assistant, provides RF engineers with an effective tool that reduces drive test analysis time from hours to minutes, while the integration of dual metrics (RSRP and SINR) enables more precise and reliable diagnostics compared to conventional single-metric methods. 
From a regulatory standpoint, this study also establishes a viable pathway for deploying Generative AI within highly regulated environments by demonstrating that quantized, locally hosted LLMs can perform advanced engineering tasks while maintaining compliance with national data privacy and residency requirements (Othman, 2025; Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission, 2024).
Limitations of the Study
Despite the successful implementation of the proposed framework, several limitations should be acknowledged. 
First, the empirical validation was confined to a single urban environment, Bandar Utama, and a specific frequency band (N78). Although the results are robust within this context, further validation is required to assess the framework’s applicability in rural settings or across higher-frequency deployments such as millimeter-wave (mmWave). 
Second, the predictive models were based solely on two-dimensional spatial features—Latitude, Longitude, and Haversine distance—without incorporating three-dimensional data such as building heights or digital elevation models (DEM), which could enhance the modeling of shadowing effects in dense urban landscapes. 
Third, the Root Cause Analysis (RCA) component relied on static, rule-based thresholds (e.g., Distance > 500 m, SINR < 0 dB), which, while effective for the N78 band, are not inherently adaptive to varying network configurations or frequency bands without manual adjustment. 
Finally, the absence of Channel Quality Indicator (CQI) data represents a notable limitation, as G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 recorded CQI values as null for 5G NR measurements. Since CQI is a user equipment (UE)-internal feedback parameter reported to the gNB scheduler and is not typically accessible through standard modem diagnostic interfaces used by third-party drive test tools (3rd Generation Partnership Project, 2023c), its inclusion was not feasible. However, incorporating CQI would have enabled a more detailed evaluation of achievable throughput and modulation schemes, complementing the RSRP-SINR analysis. Future research should therefore consider the use of professional-grade tools, such as TEMS Investigation or Nemo Outdoor, which provide deeper chipset-level access to CQI measurements.
Recommendations for Future Work
Building upon the foundation established in this thesis, several directions for future research are proposed. 
First, the integration of three-dimensional geospatial data, including building footprints and heights, would enhance the predictive capability of the model by enabling a transition from 2D Haversine distance to more accurate line-of-sight (LoS) and non-line-of-sight (NLoS) classifications (Rekkas et al., 2025). 
Second, the static thresholds currently used for Root Cause Analysis could be improved through the adoption of unsupervised learning techniques or adaptive thresholding methods that dynamically adjust to varying propagation conditions across different frequency bands and deployment scenarios. 
Third, future studies should incorporate additional performance indicators such as Channel Quality Indicator (CQI), Block Error Rate (BLER), and throughput metrics, thereby extending the framework beyond Layer 1 (physical layer) analysis to include Layer 2 (MAC layer) optimization, and enabling more comprehensive recommendations, including scheduler-level tuning (Nurakhov, 2025). 
Fourth, while the present framework operates as an open-loop decision support system, future work could explore its integration into Open Radio Access Network (O-RAN) architectures through near-real-time RAN Intelligent Controllers (RIC), allowing for closed-loop automation and direct execution of optimization strategies generated by the LLM (Azkaei, 2025). 
Finally, the incorporation of multi-modal AI approaches should be considered, where vision-language models such as Qwen-VL could be used to analyze RF heatmaps and geographical imagery directly, thereby enhancing the system’s ability to replicate the visual diagnostic reasoning employed by human engineers.
Conclusion
The optimization of 5G networks is a complex, multi-dimensional challenge that exceeds the capacity of traditional manual analysis. This thesis demonstrates that integrating geospatial machine learning with locally deployed Generative AI provides a robust and privacy-preserving approach. The proposed framework automates the detection of coverage anomalies through combined RSRP and SINR analysis, identifies their root causes using spatial and RF-based indicators, and produces targeted engineering recommendations via a local LLM. As a result, it significantly enhances both the efficiency and accuracy of RF optimization. The findings reinforce the key premise that a fully integrated, privacy-compliant dashboard—merging GIS-based spatial analysis, Random Forest prediction, and local LLM-driven reasoning—has not been formally established, and that this study successfully realizes such a framework. As telecommunications systems continue to advance in complexity and automation, the approaches introduced in this research serve as an important foundation for the development of fully autonomous and self-optimizing 5G and future 6G networks.
