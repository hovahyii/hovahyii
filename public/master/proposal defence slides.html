Background of the Study

The deployment of 5G New Radio (NR) networks has brought significant complexity into Radio Frequency (RF) optimization due to massive MIMO antenna arrays and highly dynamic signal propagation.

Historically, RF optimization has relied on manual interpretation of static coverage plots and drive test logs. While these methods effectively visualize signal strength metrics like Reference Signal Received Power (RSRP), they lack the capacity for automated contextual analysis.

"Being purely descriptive, they fail to identify the underlying root causes of performance degradation, such as pilot pollution, overshooting, or terrain shadowing (Al-Ahmed & Shakir, 2022)."

Problem Statement

1. Manual interpretation of drive test data remains a labor-intensive and time-consuming process... prone to human error, particularly when identifying transient interference phenomena such as pilot pollution.

2. Although traditional machine learning models such as Random Forest and XGBoost have demonstrated strong capabilities in predictive analytics... they lack the ability to provide prescriptive insights. These models cannot explain the underlying causes of signal degradation in natural language or recommend specific engineering actions.

3. Furthermore, the adoption of advanced AI solutions is constrained by data privacy and sovereignty requirements... Operators must comply with strict regulations that limit the use of cloud-based AI platforms like ChatGPT and Gemini due to the risk of exposing critical national infrastructure data.

Research Objectives

1.To develop a data pipeline that fuses user-plane drive test metrics (RSRP, SINR) with highly accurate, device-recorded network infrastructure data (cellinfo.txt) and geospatial environmental layers, overcoming the inaccuracy limitations of crowdsourced databases like OpenCellID.

2.To assess whether locally deployed Generative AI can provide explainable RF root cause analysis that aligns with standard human engineering judgment under data sovereignty constraints.

3.To evaluate the integration of predictive geospatial analytics and local Generative AI within a GIS-based decision-support system for RF optimization.

Scope of Research

This research is primarily focused on the optimization of Downlink (DL) coverage within LTE and 5G Non-Standalone (NSA) networks. To capture a realistic representation of the user experience, field measurement data was collected using the fully licensed G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 application installed on a standard Android 5G handset (Infinix Note 60 Pro).

Unlike previous approaches that relied on crowdsourced databases such as OpenCellID for base station locations, this research leverages the precise site coordinates recorded directly by G-NetTrack Pro v33.3 in the cellinfo.txt file, providing sub-meter accuracy for spatial fusion calculations.

The study acknowledges specific technical boundaries to ensure feasibility. It does not extend to Uplink (UL) performance optimization or core network latency analysis. All data processing, spatial computations, and generative AI reasoning strictly execute on local consumer-grade hardware to ensure compliance with the Malaysian Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010.

Literature Review (Predictive Modeling)

Maneesha & Kiran (2025)

Methodology & Findings

Evaluated ML models (Logistic Regression, SVM, Random Forest) for 5G coverage prediction. Random Forest achieved highest accuracy ($R^2=0.97$, RMSE=1.14 dB), proving robustness in noisy high-dimensional drive-test data.

Identified Limitation & Gap

Acts as a "Black Box". Accurately predicts coverage degradation numerically but provides no semantic insight into underlying causes (e.g., downtilt, clutter) and yields no actionable engineering recommendations.

Xu et al. (2025)

Methodology & Findings

Addressed ML opacity by combining XGBoost ($R^2>0.9$) and SHAP values using 3D building data. SHAP successfully identified "Distance to Site" and "Building Height" as dominant factors in path loss.

Identified Limitation & Gap

Explanation remains primarily mathematical feature extraction rather than semantic generative text. Engineers must still manually interpret the numerical values to translate them into physical actions.

Literature Review (Detection & Clustering)

Al-Ahmed & Shakir (2022) / Trujillo et al. (2025)

Methodology & Findings

Applied Semi-Supervised ML on Minimization of Drive Tests (MDT) KPIs for real-time overshoot, undershoot, and hole detection (98% precision). Overcomes fixed manual thresholds.

Identified Limitation & Gap

Excellent pattern detection but lacks diagnostic depth. Cannot classify contextual root causes (e.g., pilot pollution vs. shadowing) and provides mere alerting instead of prescriptive guidance.

Nikula et al. (2025)

Methodology & Findings

Applied DBSCAN clustering with LSTM Autoencoders to group irregular network performance degradation across complex non-spherical environments.

Identified Limitation & Gap

Focused solely on temporal KPI patterns. Neglected spatial relationships (Latitude/Longitude coordinates) required to correlate network faults with geographical environments.

Literature Review (Semantic Reasoning)

Sana et al. (2025)

Methodology & Findings

Explored the use of GPT-4 for RCA via structured prompts over domain-specific "TeleLogs". Concluded that Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting significantly reduced technical hallucination during configuration mismatch identification.

Identified Limitation & Gap
  • • Cloud Constraints: Complete reliance on cloud models directly conflicts with local data sovereignty mandates required by national telecommunications operators.
  • • Spatial Blindness: Exclusively ingests tabular log text. The absolute lack of geographic coordinate integration prevents it from reasoning about spatial interference physics.

Methodology - Layer 1: Spatial Fusion

Haversine Distance Formulation

$$a = \sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta\phi}{2}\right) + \cos\phi_1 \cdot \cos\phi_2 \cdot \sin^2\left(\frac{\Delta\lambda}{2}\right)$$ $$d = r \cdot 2 \cdot \text{atan2}(\sqrt{a}, \sqrt{1-a})$$

Phase 1 programmatically joins asynchronous telemetry logs with precise network topology using great-circle distance computations, accommodating the Earth's curvature. Essential for establishing the baseline line-of-sight signal expectation.

Methodology - Layer 2: Predictive Engine

Random Forest Regressor

Establishes the structural RF baseline. It models non-linear path loss constraints typical of dense urban multiplexing environments using purely spatial predictors (Lat/Lng/Distance) to forecast absolute RSRP.

$$\hat{y} = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} f_k(x)$$

Methodology - Layer 2: Spatial Clustering

DBSCAN Algorithm

Pivots analysis from reactive point-measurements to proactive multi-dimensional geographic areas. Identifies contiguous regions of degraded coverage without relying on predefined geometric network layouts.

RSRP >= -95 dBm

Methodology - Layer 3: Prescriptive GenAI

Context-Aware LLM Prompting

The framework translates spatial boundaries back into semantic tokens via a Chain-of-Thought template, dynamically injected with telemetry.

System: You are an RF Engineer analyzing a cluster.

Data: [Cluster ID = 5, Min SINR = -9.0dB, PCIs = 11...]

Logic flow:
1. Evaluate RSRP severity vs Distance curve.
2. Discern SINR impact vs Unique PCI counts.
3. Determine RCA -> Generate 3 tuning recommendations.

Conclusion & Future Work

This study successfully developed and validated an Integrated Geospatial-Semantic RF Optimization Framework that automates the transition from descriptive drive test data to prescriptive engineering actions. By combining the predictive power of Random Forest (R² = 0.8206) [13], the spatial clustering capabilities of DBSCAN [23], [21], and the semantic reasoning of a locally deployed Large Language Model [36], [31], the framework effectively emulates human expert judgment in diagnosing 5G network anomalies [19], [25].

The empirical results demonstrate that complex RF optimization tasks can be automated while strictly adhering to data sovereignty requirements through edge-based AI deployment [27], [11], [14], [18]. The system accurately differentiated between distance-related path loss [29], [37], severe pilot pollution [5], [33], and near-field antenna anomalies [15], [35], generating specific, actionable recommendations for each scenario.

Future work will focus on integrating this framework with Open RAN (O-RAN) architectures to enable closed-loop, automated network tuning based on the AI-generated prescriptions [6]. Furthermore, while this study was limited to a single geographic area (Bandar Utama) and relied on rule-derived labels rather than ground-truth human annotations, the methodology is highly scalable [19]. Future iterations will validate the framework across diverse urban morphologies and multi-operator environments [9]. Ultimately, this approach provides a blueprint for national operators to achieve autonomous network optimization while strictly adhering to data sovereignty mandates [27], [11].
